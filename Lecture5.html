Monte Carlo

The Monte Carlo randomization technique involves creating a large number of random data that we can then use to estimate some property of a theoretical space.
For example, we can use Monte Carlo to estimate the area of a circle by randomly, and uniformly, dropping points in an known area surrounding the circle. We can compute the area as a proportion of points inside the circle to the total area.

The purpose of randomization is to estimate some characteristic of the sampling distribution.

The bootstrap is a special case Monte Carlo where randomization is used for estimating a characteristic of the empirical data.

Normal Distributions In Biology

The normal distribution is often used to model either a population or a sample distribution.

The idea is that you sample from the population, multiple times, and on each iteration you calculate a sample mean, standard deviation and this other parameter: the Standard Error.

The standard error is meant to convey the 95% CI of the mean. But only works if the data are truly Normally distributed.

## Usage of the Normal Theory Often biologists, incorrectly, use the normal distribution to model either the population or the sample distribution. The idea is that you have some data that you sampled from some population, which you just assign as normal: central limit the. assump.

The One sample T-Test

First, we need to know the population $\mu$.

Then we can compute our sample mean, $\bar{x}$, and standard deviation, $s$.

Then we compute the $t$-statistic: $t=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}}$

$t=\frac{219,748-81,074}{\frac{407,896}{\sqrt{10}}} = 1.08$ with a pvalue of 0.31

The One sample T-Test

First, we take our sample and resample, with replacement, 10,000 times.

Then we can compute $\overset{*}{\theta_b}$, each boostrap sample mean.

Then we sort the bootstrap means and find the lower and upper 2.5% (95%CI).

We then as if $\mu$ is within the confidence interval.
