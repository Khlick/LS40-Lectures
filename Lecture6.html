Brief Review

  • We use Statistics to get at the Underlying Process of an experimental outcome.
  • We collect data in the form of observations and store them into variables.
  • The data can be either categorical or quantitative.
  • When we collect data, our collection is called the sample.
  • We use some descriptive statistic (e.g. median, mean or others) on our sample to construct the sampling distribution, an estimate of the related parameter of the underlying distribution.
  • The underlying distribution is probably the population.

Brief Review

Proportions of Proportions
  • A chance model is used to test if an observation can be predicted to have happened by random chance.
  • Computer simulation allows us to rapidly build a chance model of an expected proportion.
  • We can then take the proportion of our chance model that is as extreme and more extreme that our observed proportion.
  • The chance model histogram is our Null Distribution and represents the expected proportion if all possible outcomes are equally probable.
- The key here is that instead of just accumulating thousands of coin flips, we want to know what proportion of the time an event occurs in N observations. - What we are simulating is if female and male pups are equally likely, Null, how often does my observed proportion happen by random chance? ### All of this is part of counting categorical data.

Brief Review

Uncertainty of Proportions
  • Using a similar simulation as when constructing the Null distribution, we can simulate the uncertainty surrounding our observed statistic.
  • We put a confidence level on our range on uncertainty, typically we use 95%.
  • We construct this 95% confidence interval using resampling with replacement.
  • That is, we throw all our observations in a big box and then draw out the same number we threw in, with replacement, then calculate our statistic (proportion) again.
  • This process is called Bootstrapping and the underlying principal is that we are adding randomness to our observation in order to assess if randomness can explain our result.
  • This method uses only our actual data to understand the role of random chance given that we only have information on our actual data.
  • How does this compare to the Null distribution we constructed on the last slide? What assumptions are made here? What about in the Null?

The Null makes an assumption about all possible outcomes, e.g. they're all equally likely.
The bootstrap makes no assumptions, except that the actual data are real, and requires no assumptions about all the other outcomes.

- Bootstrap is the term we use for resampling with replacement, it is also called randomization. - Replacement means, we draw a value and stick it back in the box so that it might be drawn again in the same collection of draws. - This assumption game is important!

Brief Review

Quantitative Analysis

Statistic

  • Population mean: $\mu$
  • Sample mean: $\bar{x}$
  • Population variance: $\sigma^2$
  • Sample variance: $s^2$
  • Pop. standard deviation: $\sigma$
  • Sample standard deviation: $s$

Calculation

  • $\mu = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}x_{i}$
  • $\bar{x}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}x_{i}$
  • $\sigma^2=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(x_i-\mu)^2$
  • $s^2=\frac{1}{N-1}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(x_i-\bar{x})^2$
  • $\sigma=\sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(x_i-\mu)^2}$
  • $s=\sqrt{\frac{1}{N-1}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(x_i-\bar{x})^2}$

These statistics are known as parametric estimators because their entire purpose is to measure (metric: measure) the parameter (para: originating from) of the underlying distribution. To use them we need to satisfy some ASSUMPTIONS.

- The main thing here is figuring out what the heck mu and sigma are. - Yes, they are parameters of the population: but what does that mean? <- trigger orange box here - It means that they are the properties of population that we are trying to guess at, estimate, with our sample. - But these parameters are unique only to Gaussian Distributions: i.e. the normal functions.

Gaussian Assumptions

  • The underlying population is normally distributed.
  • The sample distribution of the mean is normally distributed.
  • The sample distributions themselves are normally distributed.
  • Each observation is independent of all others.
  • The underlying population has infinite members.
## Important to know these assumptions! In order to do something as magical as provide a specific probability for observing a particular mean or a particular difference between two means, our statistical procedures must make some assumptions. Sample distribution of mean: You took a sample, calculated its mean and wrote it down; then took another (independent) sample (from the same population) and got its mean and wrote it down; and did this an infinite number of times; then the distribution of the values that you wrote down would always be a perfect bell curve. As described by that e to -mu/sigma squared equation. In technical terms, the Assumption of Normality claims that the sampling distribution of the mean is normal or that the distribution of means across samples is normal. Example: Imagine (again) that you are interested in the average level of anxiety suffered by graduate students. Therefore, you take a group of grads (i.e., a random sample) and measure their levels of anxiety. Then you calculate the mean level of anxiety across all of the subjects. This final value is the sample mean. The Assumption of Normality says that if you repeat the above sequence many many many times and plot the sample means, the distribution would be normal. Note that I never said anything about the distribution of anxiety levels within given samples, nor did I say anything about the distribution of anxiety levels in the population that was sampled. I only said that the distribution of sample means would be normal. And again, there are two ways to express this: “the distribution of sample means is normal” and/or “the sampling distribution of the mean is normal.” Both are correct as they imply the same thing.

Displaying Quantitative data

The histogram is useful for qualitatively determining if your data follow the Normal Distribution shape. It was good for proportions, it's good for quantitative data too. We can even observe the cumulative density histogram.

A histogram includes:

  • The observed values along the X-Axis.
  • The counts of each observation ''bin'' in the Y-Axis.
  • We can draw a line that divides the histogram at the median
  • We can visually inspect the ''shape'' and modality of our data.

Displaying Quantitative data

A boxplot is a graphical display of the five number summary for a quantitative variable. It shows the general shape of the distribution, identifies the middle 50% of the data, and highlights any outliers.

A boxplot includes:

  • A box stretching from Q1 to Q3
  • A line that divides the box drawn at the median
  • A line from each quartile to the most extreme data value that is not an outlier. (if no outliers minimum and maxixum)
  • Each outlier plotted individually

Normal Distributions In Biology

The normal distribution is often used to model either a population or a sample distribution.

The idea is that you sample from the population, multiple times, and on each iteration you calculate a sample mean, standard deviation and this other parameter: the Standard Error.

The standard error is meant to convey the 95% CI of the mean. But only works if the data are truly Normally distributed.

## Usage of the Normal Theory Often biologists, incorrectly, use the normal distribution to model either the population or the sample distribution. The idea is that you have some data that you sampled from some population, which you just assign as normal: central limit the. assump.
